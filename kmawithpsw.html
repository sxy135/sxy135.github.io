KMA Redesign -
Improve efficiency and lower learning efforts of a complicated online data analytics system






Project Overview

  • About : This is a redesign project of a complicated data analytics system of Nielsen called KMA.
    We managed to integrate the old KMA system into the new Nielsen services platform.

    We also fixed the usability issues of the current system to reduce the learning efforts and to improve the efficiency.
  • My Role : UX Designer - lead the whole design process from user research, prototyping, to user testing
  • Timeline : Sep 2019 - Present, plan to launch around Mar 2021
  • Impact : Largely reduced training time, 95.8% improved user satisfaction rate, and successful integration to the Nielsen application platform



Project Timeline

This project has three phases. We started with the background research, and then we tried to define the problems by walking through the current system and getting initial feedback from the user panel.

Then, I had three rounds of design iteration to evolve from wireframe to high-fidelity prototype. In between each round, I would present my design to the product manager, the design team, the development team, and the user panel to get helpful feedback.

At present, we are in the third phase of developing the system, and I am supporting the development team to finalize screens for MVP.




What is KMA?

KMA is short for Key Measures Analyzer. It is a powerful internal tool for analysts to understand the potential success of a product through comparisons. The analysts compose competitive sets of the study data and then run comparisons to get the prediction results.


Why do we need comparisons in KMA? Because we can't tell if the raw data is good or bad without context.

For example, if a company wants to launch a new drink. They might do the marketing research get a potential customer satisfaction rate of 80%. But what does 80% indicate? We can't tell without comparing this with other data. If we could know the average satisfaction rate of all drinks is about 70%, then we can infer that product is likely to have success in the market. Of course, comparisons in KMA are much more complicated than this, but this can reflect how KMA works.

Impacts


Now it's time to revisit the four design challenges that I have defined - Efficiency, Learnability, Visibility, and Consistency. These could also be used as the metrics to measure the success in this project.

In order to understand the impacts of this redesign project, I have tried to set up some user feedback or user testing sessions with the product manager as well as send out surveys to collect relevant qualitative and quantitive data to measure the user feedback. In the meantime, I also presented the design to the product design team to get internal feedback. Based on the data I have gathered, the project has made a success in the following aspects:

1. 95.8% improvement in user satisfaction (based on the survey)

2. Minimum of 15% time saving with the MVP (based on the survey and user testing)

3. Improvement in learnability and visibility (based on the survey and user feedback session)

4. Consistencywith the design system (based on feedback from the product design team)

Reflection


1. Design with Constraints

There are many constraints like time and resources budget from the product manager, the current design system, and the technical complexities from the developments in design projects. To balance these, I tried to involve all stakeholders in an earlier phase of the design process and this helped to keep the design on the right track. However, despite different voices from all stakeholders, it's my responsibility to determine the priority of all these factors and make the final design decision.

2. Thinking Out of the Box

At the beginning of the redesign process, I thought I could just improve the interaction and visual design based on the current structure. However, as I learned more about the pain points, I realized that I have to think out of the box and redesign the whole information architecture to create a better and more intuitive user flow.

3. Systematic Thinking

The requirement of keeping consistent with the current design system and the work of defining components and patterns in the design system helps me realize the importance of systematic thinking. As a designer, I should not care about just one application, I should always design for the whole set of products.
